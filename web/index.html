A little synthetic voice that beeps and chirps like a droid from the movies — and slowly learns which of its sounds you like.

Press it to hear a fresh little sound. Nothing is saved — it just plays.

In a minute

Think of the chatty little robots in science-fiction films — they don't speak words, they beep, chirp, warble, and sigh, and somehow you still know when they're happy, worried, or done with a task. This project builds a voice like that, from scratch, on your own computer.

The twist is that the voice has taste. It can make many versions of a sound, and a second part — a little "judge" — has learned, from your thumbs-up / thumbs-down votes, which versions you actually like. So every time it reacts, you get a sound that is both fresh (never the exact same one twice) and to your taste.

Everything runs locally — no cloud, no internet, no recorded movie audio. It's all generated by maths (pure number-crunching on the computer's chip), so it's completely original. This page is just for listening; teaching it new sounds happens in a separate terminal tool.

How it works

You ask for a reaction by picking a feeling, or simply typing words — an interjection like ha or ugh, or any short phrase, sets the mood (it's never read aloud). Then three simple steps happen, left to right:

  1. Make many little sounds. A fixed sound-maker (it never changes) spins up a whole batch of candidate beeps and chirps — some longer, some brighter, some with a wobble or a metallic ring — so they genuinely differ from one another.
  2. Pick a good one. The judge — the part that learned your taste from your past votes — listens to all of them and quietly scores each. It then samples one of its top picks — not always the single best — so each time you ask you get a different good take.
  3. You hear a fresh one. That chosen sound plays. Ask again and you'll get a different good one.

A nice consequence: the judge can choose but it can never dull things down — it only ever plays one of its top-ranked picks, never a dud (a quality floor). The very first candidate is always the plain, hand-tuned sound, kept in every batch as the baseline. Because the winner is sampled from the top few — so you rarely hear the same one twice — it's always one of the best, not always the single best.

The pieces

Cases — the things it can react to

A case is simply one thing the voice can react to — a feeling, a status, or a noise — that you can pick, vote on, and hear. There are three kinds:

  • An emotion — how it feels. Curious, proud, sad.
  • An expression — what it's doing, coloured by a feeling. Done, thinking, error.
  • A vocalization — a little non-verbal noise with its own built-in shape. A laugh, a sigh, a chirp, a yes-beep or no-buzz.

The feeling map

Every case is placed on a simple feeling map using two dials. Left-to-right is pleasantness (unpleasant on the left, pleasant on the right). Bottom-to-top is energy (calm at the bottom, excited at the top). So "proud" sits up and to the right (pleasant, high-energy); "sad" sits down and to the left (unpleasant, low-energy). That position is what tells the voice how a sound should feel.

the real name This feeling map has a technical name — the valence–arousal circumplex. "Valence" is just pleasantness; "arousal" is just energy. You'll see those words on the diagram and elsewhere; they mean the two dials above.

Training your own

The voice gets its taste from you. In the terminal tool, it plays a stream of candidate sounds one at a time and you simply tap a key for each: good or bad (or skip). Those votes are the whole lesson. When you've voted enough, it learns from them, and from then on it judges new sounds the way you would.

The full cycle has five steps, and you can loop back any time to teach it more:

  1. Define a case (the thing to react to).
  2. Collect — it plays varied candidate sounds.
  3. Vote good or bad on each one.
  4. Learn — it studies your votes and saves what it learned.
  5. Use it — that learned taste now picks the sounds you hear.

Your votes are never thrown away — even "removing" one just hides it, so nothing is lost. And the voice always works even before you've taught it anything: with no votes yet, it just plays its plain hand-tuned sound. Your teaching only makes it better.

The terminal tool (droid)

This page only lets you listen. The real control — teaching the voice, testing whether it learned anything, managing saved versions — lives in a small terminal command called droid. You don't need it to enjoy the demo, but here's what it does, in plain terms:

droid say proud
Play one reaction for a case — the same thing this page's Play sound button does.
droid demo
An interactive playground in the terminal: type a case, or a "phrase [tag]", and hear it react.
droid collect <case> → droid train
Teach it your taste. It plays candidate sounds, you vote good/bad, then it learns from your votes.
droid bootstrap <voice>
Quick-start a brand-new voice from its built-in character — a rough first draft to refine by ear later.
droid abtest <voice>
The honesty test. It plays its own pick against a random one, blind — you choose which is better, and it tallies whether its taste really beats chance.
droid web
Open this page.
droid eval · models · profiles · doctor
Inspect quality (held-out score), manage saved versions, list voices, and health-check the whole stack.

Run droid with no arguments for an interactive studio, or droid <command> --help for any command's options.

For the technically curious

The plain story above is accurate; here is the same thing in the machine-learning vocabulary, for readers who want it. Nothing below is needed to use the demo.

The judge is a preference model, not a generator

An earlier design trained a model to generate the voice directly — and it flattened, because it regressed the synth's control knobs toward a dull average. The fix is borrowed from modern preference learning (Best-of-N / rejection sampling): the learned model is a judge, not a generator. It never picks knobs; it only ranks phrases the rich, frozen synth already produced. Your keep/drop votes are its entire training set. Because it can only choose among lively candidates, it can never flatten the voice. Candidate #0 is always the exact analytic phrase: a pure argmax Best-of-N would guarantee match-or-beat, but the live demo samples from the judge's top-N (softmax + a novelty penalty, for variety), so the played take is always top-ranked — a quality floor — though not necessarily the single highest-scored candidate.

The judge itself — a preference GP

Under the hood the judge is a closed-form Gaussian-process (GP) preference model over your votes, with a shared Matérn-5/2 kernel used for both training and play (so they can't drift) and a median-heuristic length-scale (a unit length-scale collapses in the high-dimensional feature space). It's MLX-native on Apple Silicon with a numpy fallback, and it's persisted as plain numpy so the play path needs no MLX. It's conditioned on the (valence, arousal) context, so a sparse case borrows strength from the votes of nearby cases on the feeling map. No torch, no sklearn.

Selection — stochastic Best-of-N with novelty

Instead of always taking the single top-scored candidate (the argmax), selection samples: it restricts to the top-N by judge score (a quality floor), softmax-samples among them at a per-case-type temperature, and subtracts a novelty penalty for similarity — in the judge's own standardized feature space — against a small ring of recent plays. The result is judge-approved and a fresh take each time. temperature 0 collapses sampling to the deterministic argmax (kept for byte-stable cues + tests). A sibling routine, diverse_set, farthest-point-picks N mutually-distinct takes for a deliberately spread set.

The AUC gate — how it knows it actually learned

Training reports held-out pairwise ranking accuracy, reported as AUC: over every (keep, drop) pair the judge never saw during fitting, how often does it score the keep above the drop? 0.50 means no signal was learned; higher means the judge generalizes to your ear. A new version is saved only if it clears the gate (or you force-save).

The shipped voice is our own numpy, on-device. Your ear is the only ground truth.
